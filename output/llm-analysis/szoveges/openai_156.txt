Black responds in kind with pawn to d5, leading to a symmetrical position from which both players must decide how to develop their pieces.

By move three, White develops the light-square bishop to f4, a natural developing move but one that cedes away a bit of central control down the line. Then, on Black’s fourth move, the knight springs to c6, leaning into the Nimzo-Indian Defense. Meanwhile, White hurries their c-pawn up the board to c4, keeping up the pressure to control the center. The position might seem calm, but just underneath, it’s a seething cauldron of potential tactics.

When we reach move eight, Black finds an opportunity through kingside castling. This sensible choice places their king in safety while connecting the rooks, but watch closely on White’s next move: pawn to a3 prepares for an eventual pawn expansion on the queenside. Already, there are hints of a tense middlegame brewing.

The turning point in this intense game is a dramatic one, as Black blunders massively with pawn to g5 on move thirty-six. This blunder throws away the defense and allows White to capitalize on a potential mating net. A safer alternative would have been to advance the king or perhaps consider safer pawn moves to solidify the position and delay White’s potential threats. The position is now primed for White to seize victory.

In the climactic final moves, White maneuvers deftly to capitalize on Black's weakened position.
